Six Methods on Record

Each entry below is an active phishing technique. Open a case file to see how it works and the red flags that expose it.

The classic and still most common method. Attackers send mass emails impersonating banks, delivery services, or well-known companies, pushing the reader to click a link or open an attachment before "it's too late."

  • Urgent subject lines: "Your account will be suspended"
  • Generic greeting instead of your real name
  • Sender address that almost matches the real one, but isn't
  • Link text says one thing, but the actual URL underneath is different

A targeted version of email phishing. Instead of a mass blast, the attacker researches one specific person — your name, employer, coworkers, even recent events in your life — to make the message feel personal and trustworthy.

  • References real details about you or your workplace
  • Appears to come from a real colleague or manager
  • Requests something unusual: a wire transfer, credentials, a "quick favor"
  • Creates time pressure so you act before verifying

Phishing delivered through text messages, often impersonating delivery notifications, banks, or government agencies. Because texts feel more personal and immediate, people tend to trust them more than email.

  • Short link services (bit.ly, tinyurl) hiding the real destination
  • "Package delivery failed, confirm your address" style bait
  • Messages claiming to be from a bank asking you to "verify" by tapping a link
  • Sent from a number you don't recognize, sometimes spoofed to look local

Phishing over a phone call. The attacker impersonates tech support, a bank representative, or even a government official, using authority and urgency to extract information or push you into installing remote-access software.

  • Caller creates panic: "suspicious activity on your account"
  • Asks you to read back a one-time verification code
  • Pressures you to stay on the line and not hang up to "verify independently"
  • Requests remote access to your computer or phone

The attacker copies a real, previously delivered email almost exactly — same layout, same sender name — but swaps the legitimate link or attachment for a malicious one, then resends it claiming to be an "updated" version.

  • You receive what looks like a duplicate of an email you already got
  • Framed as a resend, correction, or updated attachment
  • Sender name matches, but the email address is slightly off on close inspection
  • The only real difference is the link or file destination

A spear-phishing attack aimed specifically at high-value targets — executives, finance officers, or business owners — usually designed to authorize a large payment or release sensitive company data.

  • Impersonates a CEO, CFO, or board member
  • Requests urgent, confidential wire transfers outside normal process
  • Often timed around travel or known absences of the real executive
  • Avoids company-wide communication channels to dodge internal checks

Standard Protective Measures

Habits that neutralize nearly every method documented above.

01

Hover or long-press any link before tapping to preview the real destination URL.

02

Never enter credentials after clicking a link from an email, text, or call — open the site yourself from a bookmark or search.

03

Treat urgency as a warning sign, not a reason to rush. Real institutions rarely demand instant action.

04

Verify unusual requests from colleagues or executives through a second channel — a call, not a reply.

05

Enable multi-factor authentication everywhere it's offered, so a stolen password alone isn't enough.

06

Report suspicious messages to your IT or security team instead of just deleting them — it helps flag the campaign.
